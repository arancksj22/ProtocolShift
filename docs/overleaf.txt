\title{ProtocolShift: An Empirical Framework for REST vs. gRPC, Isolating Protocol Metrics Across Multi-Paradigm Cloud (AWS) Database Architectures}

\author{\IEEEauthorblockN{Shlok Doshi (B039), Moksh Jain (B054), Aranck Jomraj (B057), Kahaan Kakabalia (B058)}}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
ProtocolShift is a high-concurrency experimental testbed that isolates the performance difference between HTTP/1.1 REST and gRPC by separating transport and serialization overhead from application logic and database I/O. The study spans a multi-database topology (PostgreSQL, Redis, and MongoDB) to measure the serialization tax, head-of-line blocking, and p95/p99 tail latencies under load. Using a purpose-built closed-loop asynchronous load generator, we run five independent trials per configuration and report 95 percent confidence intervals, coefficient of variation, and Mann-Whitney U significance tests rather than single-run averages. On a two-node AWS deployment, gRPC reduces p99 latency by 88 to 94 percent relative to REST across all three backends under concurrency, with disjoint confidence intervals and p less than 0.001. Query-level profiling with pg\_stat\_statements shows database execution accounts for only 0 to 2 percent of request time, confirming that the measured gap is protocol overhead and not storage. We further show that an earlier reported PostgreSQL anomaly, in which gRPC appeared slower than REST, was an artifact of process-per-request load generation and disappears under a persistent-channel client. The results are distilled into a three-gate Protocol Migration Threshold model that turns the migration decision into a reproducible, data-driven rule.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Distributed Systems, gRPC, REST, Observability, Tail Latency, Database Architecture

\section{Introduction}
The choice between Remote Procedure Call and RESTful communication is a foundational engineering decision with large consequences for scalability and resource allocation. REST over HTTP/1.1 with JavaScript Object Notation (JSON) has long been the default for inter-service communication \cite{vural2017}, but its verbose, text-parsed payloads can consume 10 to 40 percent of response time under peak load. gRPC, built on HTTP/2 binary multiplexing and Protocol Buffers, offers large theoretical gains through HPACK header compression, binary encoding, and the removal of HTTP/1.1 head-of-line blocking \cite{nguyen2022, kirschner2023}.

Despite the industry enthusiasm, migrations from REST to gRPC are usually undertaken without a statistically grounded framework for deciding when migration is warranted. We identify five critical research gaps:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Database blind spot.} Most benchmarks measure over-the-wire latency but never test whether database latency dominates the request path, ignoring Amdahl's Law \cite{kaur2023}.
\item \textbf{Incomplete tail-latency results.} The literature reports mean latency; p99 comparisons under CPU or garbage-collection stress are largely absent \cite{dean2013}.
\item \textbf{No migration thresholds.} Benchmarks show gRPC is faster in isolation but set no numerical limits that justify the engineering cost of switching.
\item \textbf{Unmeasured migration cost.} The performance cost of a dual-stack architecture during a live transition is not quantified.
\item \textbf{No local-to-cloud coefficient.} Local Docker benchmarks are assumed to generalize, ignoring cloud jitter and the overhead of mutual TLS.
\end{itemize}

We address these gaps with ProtocolShift, a controlled, richly observable testbed. Our contributions are: a tripartite storage benchmark comparing REST and gRPC on relational (PostgreSQL), document (MongoDB), and in-memory (Redis) backends; a fully asynchronous, zero-ORM pipeline tuned to expose raw serialization and transport cost; a statistically rigorous evaluation with repeated trials, confidence intervals, and significance testing; and a metric-validated Protocol Migration Threshold model.

\section{Literature Review}
\subsection{Microservice Communication}
REST APIs have been the default for inter-service communication since Lewis and Fowler formalized microservices \cite{vural2017}. Systematic reviews nonetheless report a shortage of empirical evidence on architecture-level design, monitoring, and live migration \cite{li2021, waseem2020, cerny2021}.

\subsection{Protocol Performance Benchmarks}
Benchmarks generally favor gRPC. Krasniewski et al. found gRPC efficient at low to medium load but memory-bound at high load \cite{krasniewski2025}. Gorton reported gRPC 15 to 40 times higher throughput on large payloads, while REST can win on small payloads locally \cite{gorton2023}. Others confirm gRPC speedups in supply-chain and data-lookup workloads, though REST remains less CPU intensive \cite{lindqvist2022, nilsson2023, apriansyah2024}. A shared limitation is that these studies test protocols without varying the storage tier, leaving open whether protocol speed matters when database latency dominates.

\subsection{HTTP/2, Serialization, and Tail Latency}
gRPC relies on HTTP/2 multiplexing to remove the head-of-line blocking of HTTP/1.1 \cite{nguyen2022}, though Marx et al. showed a single HTTP/2 connection can bottleneck on poor networks \cite{marx2018}. Protobuf serializes faster and smaller than JSON, encoding number-heavy payloads up to 34 times faster \cite{proos2020, kirschner2023}, at the cost of schema discipline \cite{sikora2016}. On tail latency, Dean and Barroso showed that fanning one request to 100 servers gives a 63 percent chance of hitting a slow node, making p95/p99 the correct metric \cite{dean2013, aerospike2025, chen2025}. Published REST-versus-gRPC comparisons at p99 under scale remain scarce.

\subsection{Migration and Observability}
The Strangler Fig pattern is the standard for incremental modernization \cite{li2020, designgurus2025}, but existing implementations rely on human rollback decisions. Prometheus and Grafana are the industry standard for the four golden signals of latency, traffic, errors, and saturation \cite{beyer2016, barrett2025}, and underpin canary deployments with automated rollback \cite{sreschool2026, rootly2025}. ProtocolShift reuses this stack to quantify a latency-triggered migration threshold.

\section{System Architecture and Methodology}
ProtocolShift is a controlled experimental testbed whose central priority is separating protocol performance from application logic and cloud noise. The same five operations (Create, Read, ReadAll, Update, Delete) are exposed through two parallel service suites over three database paradigms.

\centerline{\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{paper_figs/architecture.jpg}}
\caption{System architecture: parallel REST and gRPC suites over three database paradigms, instrumented by Prometheus and Grafana.}

\subsection{Service Suites}
The REST suite uses FastAPI on the Asynchronous Server Gateway Interface, with Pydantic enforcing JSON schemas. The asynchronous event loop ensures that saturation is measured from connection limits and payload cost rather than thread-pool exhaustion. The gRPC suite implements the identical methods with grpc.aio, carrying data over persistent HTTP/2 streams with binary framing. Both suites share an identical storage layer and key scheme so that the only variable is the protocol.

\subsection{Multi-Paradigm Database Testbeds}
PostgreSQL 16 is the heavy-compute relational tier, accessed through raw asyncpg with no ORM. Redis 7 is the in-memory tier that removes disk I/O and gives the cleanest measure of transport efficiency. MongoDB 7 is the document tier that stresses variable-length payload handling. Using three paradigms lets us test directly whether protocol optimization is over-engineering when the database dominates.

\subsection{Load Generation and Statistical Method}
Earlier iterations of this work generated load with per-request command-line tools, which spawned a new process and a new HTTP/2 connection for every gRPC call. This discarded the multiplexing advantage of gRPC and distorted results. We replaced it with a closed-loop asynchronous load generator: REST traffic flows through a single persistent httpx connection pool, and gRPC traffic through one persistent grpc.aio channel, matching production usage.

Each configuration is a cell defined by suite, backend, concurrency in \{10, 50, 100\}, and payload size in \{100 B, 1 KB, 10 KB\}. Every cell runs five independent trials of 15 seconds each after a 3 second warm-up, under a mixed create and read workload, with the database flushed between trials so every trial starts identically. Per-request latencies are recorded client-side. We report the trial-level mean with a 95 percent Student t confidence interval, the coefficient of variation, and a Mann-Whitney U test on the pooled per-request distributions, because latency distributions are heavy-tailed and not normal. Confidence intervals are computed across trials rather than across autocorrelated per-request samples.

\subsection{The Protocol Migration Threshold}
We formalize the migration decision as three gates that must all pass for a service tier at its representative load:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Improvement gate:} gRPC reduces p99 latency by at least 20 percent relative to REST.
\item \textbf{Robustness gate:} the two p99 confidence intervals are disjoint and the Mann-Whitney U test rejects equality at alpha equal to 0.01.
\item \textbf{Amdahl gate:} database execution time is below 60 percent of total request time, measured with pg\_stat\_statements. If the database dominates, protocol migration cannot yield macroscopic gains.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Observability and Orchestration}
Prometheus scrapes dedicated telemetry endpoints on every container; each gRPC service publishes histograms from a secondary HTTP server multiplexed into its asyncio loop. Latency is bucketed from 0.001 to 2.5 seconds for precise percentile estimation. The full topology runs under Docker Compose on isolated bridge networks, with shared environment anchors guaranteeing that the REST and gRPC layers see identical variables, and health checks gating startup for reproducibility.

\section{Experimental Setup and Reproducibility}
The cloud testbed was deployed on Amazon EC2 in the ap-south-1 region across two t3.medium nodes, each with 2 vCPUs (Intel Xeon, Skylake), 4.0 GiB RAM, and a 20 GiB gp3 volume, running Ubuntu Server 24.04 LTS with Docker Engine 26.1 and Python 3.12.3. One node hosted the service stack and databases; the second acted as the load generator, communicating over the internal VPC subnet to remove public-internet routing. Every campaign captures host hardware, Docker image digests, and dependency versions to an environment manifest so that any reported number is traceable to an exact configuration. Connection pools of 2 to 10 persistent sockets are opened at startup so that TCP handshakes to the database are excluded from measured latency.

\section{Experimental Results}
All values below are p99 latency in milliseconds with 95 percent confidence intervals across five trials. Every REST-versus-gRPC comparison reported as significant has disjoint intervals and Mann-Whitney U p less than 0.001.

\subsection{gRPC Dominates Across All Backends Under Load}
Figure \ref{fig:bars} summarizes the headline result at concurrency 100 with a 1 KB payload. gRPC reduces p99 latency by roughly 90 percent on every backend: PostgreSQL 1705 ms to 159 ms, MongoDB 2151 ms to 222 ms, and Redis 1988 ms to 143 ms. The REST suite saturates near 250 to 310 requests per second while gRPC sustains 1300 to 1450, a direct consequence of HTTP/2 multiplexing avoiding the connection-pool exhaustion that throttles HTTP/1.1.

\subsection{The PostgreSQL Anomaly Was a Load-Generation Artifact}
An earlier version of this study reported gRPC as slower than REST on PostgreSQL, with a cloud p99 of 861 ms against 347 ms. That result was produced by a load generator that opened a new process and connection per request. Under the persistent-channel generator, the direction reverses completely: gRPC is 88 to 92 percent faster than REST on PostgreSQL under load, for example 891 ms to 80 ms at concurrency 50 and 1852 ms to 141 ms at concurrency 100 with a 100 B payload. The anomaly was therefore a measurement artifact, not a property of the protocol or the async driver. Figure \ref{fig:pgprofile} shows the corrected scaling behavior from a dedicated profiled run.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{paper_figs/pg_profiled_scaling.png}}
\caption{PostgreSQL p99 vs concurrency under the persistent-channel generator. gRPC is consistently faster, reversing the earlier anomaly.}
\label{fig:pgprofile}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Query Profiling Confirms Protocol, Not Database, Dominance}
To test the Amdahl gate directly, a profiled PostgreSQL campaign captured per-query execution time with pg\_stat\_statements. Across all cells, database execution accounted for only 0 to 2 percent of total request time: individual INSERT and SELECT statements averaged well under 0.3 ms, while end-to-end request p99 ranged from tens to thousands of milliseconds. This confirms that for these CRUD workloads the bottleneck is transport and queueing, not storage, which is exactly why a faster protocol produces large end-to-end gains. It also demonstrates the database-blind-spot risk in reverse: because the database share is far below the 60 percent Amdahl threshold, migration is justified on this tier.

\subsection{In-Memory Tier and a Resource-Exhaustion Limitation}
The Redis 10 KB high-concurrency cells were re-run after a resource failure described in Section VII. Under the corrected run, gRPC reduces Redis p99 from 821 ms to 61 ms at concurrency 50 and from 2303 ms to 126 ms at concurrency 100, improvements of 92.5 and 94.6 percent (Figure \ref{fig:redis}). Notably, under the two-node deployment the p50 latencies of Redis and PostgreSQL under gRPC are nearly identical, because the inter-node round-trip and service overhead, not the storage engine, set the latency floor.

\subsection{Migration Threshold Verdicts}
Applying the three-gate model to the profiled PostgreSQL data yields MIGRATE at concurrency 50 and 100 for all payloads, where improvement exceeds 89 percent, intervals are disjoint, and the database share is near zero. At concurrency 10 with 1 KB and 10 KB payloads the verdict is HOLD, because although gRPC is faster the confidence intervals overlap and the robustness gate fails. The model thus prevents an over-eager migration recommendation at low load while endorsing it under the concurrency where it matters.

\section{Discussion}
The results validate Amdahl's Law in a communication context. gRPC consistently outperforms REST because HTTP/2 multiplexing removes head-of-line blocking and binary serialization avoids text parsing, but the magnitude depends on how much of the request path the protocol actually occupies. The profiling data make this concrete: when the database share is near zero, protocol choice governs latency, and gRPC wins decisively. The corrected PostgreSQL result carries a broader methodological lesson, namely that a flawed load generator can invert a benchmark conclusion, so client instrumentation must reproduce production connection behavior. Finally, the Strangler-Fig-inspired threshold model shows that a safe migration boundary can be derived directly from measured tail latency and query-time share rather than from vendor claims.

\section{Limitations}
\subsection{Redis Memory Exhaustion}
The disk-backed backends completed their full sweeps, but the initial cloud campaign lost 14 of 90 Redis trials during the 10 KB sweeps at concurrency 50 and 100. Because that campaign disabled inter-trial flushing to preserve cross-node consistency, several hundred thousand records, including roughly 90000 10 KB payloads, accumulated in memory. On the 4.0 GiB node this exhausted physical RAM, and behavior consistent with kernel out-of-memory termination stopped the Redis container, producing connection resets. A single surviving contaminated trial recorded a p99 of about 4.3 seconds and was excluded from all comparisons; the analysis pipeline now automatically excludes any cell with fewer than two completed trials. The re-run with per-trial flushing kept Redis memory flat and completed cleanly, and the reported Redis numbers come from that corrected run. This episode is itself a finding: in-memory stores are well suited to small payloads but require strict eviction policies when used for large payloads under sustained write load.

\subsection{Security Overhead Not Measured}
All experiments ran over plaintext HTTP/1.1 and HTTP/2 without transport encryption. Production gRPC commonly relies on TLS or mutual TLS, whose handshake and per-message costs are not captured here. Because our results attribute the latency gap to serialization and multiplexing, mutual TLS is a plausible equalizer at low payload sizes and is a stated limitation rather than a measured quantity.

\subsection{Shared-Host and Single-Region Scope}
One backend was exercised at a time while the others idled, so the relative REST-versus-gRPC comparison is clean, but the absolute numbers reflect a shared-host environment. All cloud measurements are from a single region and instance type, so the local-to-cloud coefficient should not be extrapolated to other topologies without re-measurement.

\section{Conclusion and Future Scope}
ProtocolShift demonstrates that protocol migration decisions should be driven by measured tail latency and query-time share rather than by isolated throughput claims. Across a relational, a document, and an in-memory backend on a two-node AWS deployment, gRPC reduces p99 latency by 88 to 94 percent under concurrency, with statistically significant, reproducible margins, and profiling confirms the gap is protocol overhead rather than storage. We also showed that a previously reported PostgreSQL anomaly was a load-generation artifact. Future work will add GraphQL for a three-way comparison of over-fetching and under-fetching, measure the computational cost of mutual TLS over gRPC streams, and implement an automated latency-triggered rollback within the Strangler Fig gateway.
